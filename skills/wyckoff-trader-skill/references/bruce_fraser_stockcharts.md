# Bruce Fraser StockCharts

This file distills the most reusable lessons from Bruce Fraser's localized 243-article StockCharts archive. Use it when you want applied Wyckoff case-study guidance, especially around phase interpretation, Point & Figure work, trend stride, rotation, and stock selection.

## Why This Source Matters

The book gives the formal grammar of Wyckoff. Bruce Fraser's archive shows how that grammar behaves in live chart work over many years, across stocks, indexes, commodities, and macro themes.

Its strongest contributions are:

- context-first reading
- phase progression and tape reading
- trend stride and channel logic
- Point & Figure counting practice and heuristics
- relative strength and rotation detection
- top-down stock selection
- campaign-style thinking for large causes

## 1. Context Is King

One of the archive's clearest messages is that schematics are road maps, not rigid templates.

Use implications:

- labels should orient the story, not dominate it
- phase distinctions matter because tactics change with phase
- the same surface pattern can have different meaning in different background conditions

Operationally:

- decide whether you are in stop, cause-building, final test, emergence, or trend
- only then decide whether a spring, UTAD, LPS, or LPSY is meaningful

## 2. Phase Analysis Is Tactical

The Fraser archive repeatedly treats phases as a tactical framework:

- `A`: stopping action
- `B`: time and cause construction
- `C`: final test
- `D`: emergence within the range
- `E`: trend out of the range

Why this matters:

- in A/B, patience and observation dominate
- in C, risk/reward can be best but false certainty is dangerous
- in D, confirmation quality improves
- in E, continuation logic matters more than bottom-picking logic

## 3. Tape Reading Is A Mastery Skill

Fraser emphasizes bar-by-bar reading rather than indicator dependence.

Focus on:

- spread quality
- close location
- volume expansion or contraction
- reaction quality after strong bars
- whether a key level is truly being accepted or merely touched

This is especially useful when a structure is ambiguous and labels alone are not enough.

## 4. Trend Stride Matters

A major Fraser contribution is the repeated use of trend stride:

- define the trend channel early
- observe whether price respects its pace
- treat throwovers and oversold/overbought excursions as important tactical clues

Implications:

- a dynamic markup should travel in a broad, persistent stride
- return into a broken stride can warn of changing character
- throwovers often coincide with exhaustion, but require confirmation

## 5. Creek And Ice Are Zones, Not Lines

Fraser's treatments of `Jumping the Creek` and `breaking the Ice` are especially practical.

Key idea:

- support and resistance are often variable zones made of repeated turning points, not exact linear prices

Bullish use:

- a proper jump across the creek should show dynamic demand and follow-through
- backups should stay constructive and not deeply violate the jump area

Bearish use:

- after the ice breaks, rallies back into it should be poor quality

## 6. Point & Figure Is Not Just For Targets

In this corpus, Point & Figure is used for:

- measuring cause
- comparing segments
- validating or questioning structure
- spotting rotation
- seeing large objectives that time-based charts can obscure

Important Fraser-style heuristics:

- practice PnF repeatedly until count logic becomes intuitive
- check smaller counts against larger background counts
- use segmented counts rather than one monolithic count when structure suggests it
- undercount distribution rather than overcount it
- in some distributions, counting from `LPSY` back to `ST` can be more conservative and more useful than counting back to the full climax
- modified scaling and percent scaling can help with very large structures

## 7. Stepping-Stone Structures Deserve Special Respect

The archive repeatedly studies:

- stepping-stone reaccumulation
- stepping-stone redistribution

Important nuance:

- these pauses often create the best tactical opportunities
- they can look deceptively weak or strong on shape alone
- repeated tests, lower peaks, or apparent weakness can still be absorptive in an SSR context

## 8. Relative Strength Should Be In Gear

Fraser repeatedly uses relative-strength ratios to rank candidates.

Preferred condition:

- price above a rising long-term average
- relative strength versus peer group above a rising average
- relative strength versus market benchmark above a rising average

This `in gear` condition helps identify leadership and persistence.

Practical implication:

- a structurally valid long is better when the asset also leads its group and benchmark
- a structurally weak short is better when the asset lags its group and benchmark

## 9. Rotation Often Starts Before It Becomes Obvious

Fraser uses Point & Figure and ratio work to detect capital rotation.

Workflow:

1. assess the broad market
2. identify sector or theme rotation
3. drill down to industry group
4. isolate the best or worst stocks inside that group

This is useful for crypto too, with the buckets changed to:

- Bitcoin
- large caps
- mid caps
- low caps
- thematic groups

## 10. Campaign Thinking Requires A Bigger Cause

Campaign-worthy setups have recurring traits in this archive:

- large, well-formed cause
- clear change of character
- successful final test
- emerging relative strength
- durable trend stride
- enough PnF potential to justify a long hold

Campaign logic is not just “big target.” It also requires the psychology and structure to stay with a persistent trend.

## 11. Practical Fraser-Style Checklist

When using this corpus to support a scenario, ask:

1. What phase is this market actually in?
2. What is the quality of the latest bars at key levels?
3. Is the stride of trend healthy, overextended, or broken?
4. Is support/resistance better understood as a creek/ice zone?
5. What does Point & Figure say about cause, segmentation, and larger background potential?
6. Is this a stepping-stone structure or a terminal one?
7. Is the asset in gear on absolute and relative terms?
8. Is there a top-down reason this candidate should lead?
