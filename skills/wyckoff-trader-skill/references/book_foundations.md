# Book Foundations

This file distills the formal framework from `The Wyckoff Methodology in Depth`. It should be treated as the canonical base layer for structure, phases, events, and trade-location logic.

## 1. How Markets Move

### Waves

- Price moves in waves, not in straight lines.
- Waves are fractal: lower-grade waves form part of higher-grade waves.
- Wave comparison is more important than isolated bars.
- Good reading comes from comparing duration, speed, and range between successive waves.

### Price Cycle

- The canonical cycle is accumulation -> uptrend -> distribution -> downtrend.
- Reaccumulation and redistribution are pauses inside larger trends, not fresh primary cycle starts.
- A market is in a `buying position` when it is in accumulation or uptrend.
- A market is in a `selling position` when it is in distribution or downtrend.
- A market is in a `neutral position` when no purposeful campaign is evident.

### Trend Assessment

Use comparison, not absolutes:

- `speed`: faster trend movement implies strength; slowing angle implies weakening
- `projection`: impulse travel should exceed prior impulse travel in a healthy trend
- `depth`: retracements should stay contained in a healthy trend
- `channels`: trendline, overbought line, oversold line, and reverse lines help frame exhaustion and support/resistance

## 2. Trading Ranges

- Not every range is accumulation or distribution; some are random balance and should be ignored.
- A purposeful range requires time because cause must be built before effect can appear.
- The range boundaries matter more than its midpoint.
- The decisive move cannot happen until professionals have both control and a clear path of least resistance.

## 3. The Three Laws

### Supply And Demand

- Price does not rise because there are "more buyers than sellers" in a naive headcount sense.
- Price rises when aggressive demand consumes available supply and forces the auction higher.
- Price falls when aggressive supply consumes available demand and forces the auction lower.
- Absence of opposing interest can move price surprisingly far with relatively little effort.

### Cause And Effect

- Trends do not come from nowhere; they are prepared by prior lateral cause-building.
- The longer and more meaningful the cause, the larger the potential effect.
- Point-and-figure charts are the preferred classical tool for translating cause into target hypotheses.

### Effort And Result

- `effort` is volume; `result` is price progress.
- Harmony between price and volume supports continuation.
- Divergence between price and volume warns of absorption, exhaustion, or hidden opposition.
- High effort with poor result is often the most useful clue near important levels.

## 4. Structural Types

### Accumulation

- Preceded by a downtrend.
- Purpose: transfer stock from weak hands to strong hands.
- Desired outcome: later markup once supply has been sufficiently absorbed.

### Reaccumulation

- A pause within an existing uptrend.
- It should be judged in the context of the prior trend, not as a fresh standalone bottom.

### Distribution

- Preceded by an uptrend.
- Purpose: transfer stock from strong hands to weaker late buyers before markdown.

### Redistribution

- A pause within an existing downtrend.
- Interpret it as continuation unless strong contrary evidence appears.

## 5. Events

### Phase-A Stop Events

- `PS / PSY`: first meaningful professional opposition to the old trend
- `SC / BC`: climactic stopping action
- `AR`: automatic counter-move after climax
- `ST`: test of the climax area

### Phase-C Test Events

- `spring`: downside shake below support in accumulation-family structures
- `UTAD`: upside shake above resistance in distribution-family structures
- `LPS / LPSY` can also serve as the turning foothold in the no-spring variant

### Phase-D / E Trend Events

- `SOS / SOW`: decisive emergence of strength or weakness
- `JAC / BUEC`: jump across the creek and back-up in bullish structures
- `FTI / LPSY`: fall through the ice and bearish confirmation behavior

## 6. Phases

### Phase A

- Stops the previous trend.
- The key question is whether genuine stopping action has appeared.

### Phase B

- Builds the cause.
- Multiple tests and deceptions can occur.
- This phase is where patience matters most.

### Phase C

- The decisive test area.
- May occur through a classic spring/UTAD or via a no-shake foothold variant.

### Phase D

- Trend within the range.
- Best confirmation area once intent becomes clearer.

### Phase E

- Trend out of the range.
- Usually lower reward-to-risk than Phase C but higher confidence.

## 7. Trade Locations

The book emphasizes only a few meaningful operating zones:

- Phase C shock area
- Phase C shock test
- Phase D trend movement inside the range
- Phase D breakout test / confirmation event
- Phase E continuation tests or minor structures aligned with the larger move

Tradeoff:

- earlier entry = more potential reward, less confirmation
- later entry = more confirmation, less remaining travel

## 8. Significant Bar And Reversal Of Movement

The significant bar is one of the most actionable concepts in the book.

Characteristics:

- wider range than prior bars
- volume proportional to that larger range
- closes in the direction of the move
- commits through a relevant support or resistance level

Implication:

- a significant bar signals intent and probable professional participation
- as long as price respects the last significant bar, control likely remains with that side
- reversal becomes more credible when an opposing significant bar breaks the prior one

## 9. Scenario Implications For This Skill

When using this skill to build scenarios:

- identify cycle position before pattern labels
- compare impulses and reactions instead of narrating single candles
- distinguish random balance from campaign structure
- favor Phase C / D / confirmation setups over undisciplined breakout chasing
- make effort/result language explicit around key levels
