# Uncommon Concepts

This file focuses on ideas that are either unusually useful, easy to forget, or not part of basic retail-charting knowledge.

## 1. Path Of Least Resistance Is Verified, Not Assumed

Wyckoff logic does not assume direction from hope or opinion. The large operator tests whether supply or demand is actually absent enough to permit movement. Until that path is verified, the campaign is incomplete.

## 2. Buying Position / Selling Position / Neutral Position

The book explicitly frames market stance this way:

- `buying position`: accumulation or markup context
- `selling position`: distribution or markdown context
- `neutral position`: no clear campaign or no actionable structure

This is more useful than vague bullish/bearish sentiment language.

## 3. Random Range vs Purposeful Range

Not every sideways market contains professional intent. Some ranges are just balance. If there is no persuasive cause-building behavior, the correct action is often to ignore the range.

## 4. Speed, Projection, And Depth

Trend health should be compared through:

- `speed`: angle and urgency of movement
- `projection`: how far impulses travel
- `depth`: how much reactions retrace

This gives a much richer read than trendlines alone.

## 5. Significant Bar

One of the strongest book concepts for scenario work:

- unusually wide range
- meaningful volume
- strong close in the intended direction
- commits beyond a relevant level

Treat it as evidence of intent, then watch whether it remains in control.

## 6. Reversal Of Movement

A reversal becomes more credible when the market breaks the control established by the last significant bar with an opposing bar of intent. This prevents premature “top/bottom calling.”

## 7. No-Shake Phase C Variant

Phase C does not always arrive as a dramatic spring or UTAD. Sometimes the range resolves from an LPS/LPSY-type foothold without a classic terminal shake. This matters when a structure looks constructive but never prints the ideal textbook spring.

## 8. ST As mSOS / ST As mSOW

A secondary test inside Phase B can already reveal directional character:

- `ST as mSOS`: secondary test that behaves like a bullish minor sign of strength
- `ST as mSOW`: secondary test that behaves like a bearish minor sign of weakness

These are subtle but powerful modifiers of the Wyckoff story.

## 9. Jump Across The Creek / Back Up To The Edge Of The Creek

Useful shorthand:

- `JAC / SOS`: the decisive bullish break
- `BUEC / LPS`: the bullish confirmation retest

The same logic applies bearishly with `ice`, `SOW`, and the confirmation test.

## 10. Fall Through The Ice

The book mentions `FTI` as the bearish analog of a break-confirmation sequence. This term is less common in casual Wyckoff discussion but useful when describing bearish confirmation logic.

## 11. Action -> Test -> Confirmation

The archive reinforces this repeatedly. The first move is not the whole setup. High-quality scenario language should make clear:

- what the first action was
- whether a real test occurred
- what would count as confirmation

## 12. Failed Signal

In the crypto archive, failed signals are treated as highly informative:

- failed upthrust
- failed short trigger
- failed spring

The failure often reveals the stronger side faster than the original setup did.

## 13. Feather

Archive-specific pattern:

- higher highs or creeping advance into resistance
- decreasing volume
- compression rather than obvious breakout force

Interpretation:

- buyers are accepting higher prices
- supply is being absorbed
- acceleration may follow if the structure is in the right background

## 14. Principle In The Principle

A smaller sub-range nested inside a larger setup. This is useful for refining timing without abandoning the higher-timeframe structure.

## 15. Bitcoin As Source Of Funding

During exogenous shocks, Bitcoin can be sold for liquidity even if its long-term structure remains constructive. This prevents mislabeling panic liquidation as pure distribution.

## 16. Risk-Off Refuge Hierarchy In Crypto

The archive suggests a practical order:

- Tether / stable refuge
- Bitcoin
- only later rotation back into larger altcoins and then smaller speculative names

## 17. Spread Charts As Alert Mechanisms

Use `ETHBTC`, `LINKBTC`, and similar charts to detect leadership changes. Do not confuse “relative outperformance” with “absolute long setup” until the USD or perp chart confirms it.

## 18. Three Stages Of Uptrend

Applied in the archive:

- value buying or initial momentum
- absorption
- speculation

This helps distinguish sustainable trend development from late-stage excitement.

## 19. Structural Analog

Historical analogs should transfer behavior, not shape. The right question is:

- “Does this market solve similar pressure the same way?”

not:

- “Is this chart identical?”

## 20. Labeling Is The Last Step

One of the best archive reminders:

- first read price and volume
- then tell the story
- only after that apply Wyckoff labels

When labels lead the analysis, the scenario usually degrades into curve-fitting.

## 21. Context Is King

Bruce Fraser's archive repeatedly sharpens this idea:

- schematics are road maps, not clocks
- the same event label can imply different tactics depending on where the structure sits in its campaign

## 22. Stride Of Trend

A trend often sets its pace early. Monitoring whether price respects, throws over, or falls back inside that stride can be more revealing than raw bullish or bearish narration.

## 23. Creek / Ice As Variable Zones

Resistance and support often behave like meandering zones rather than exact prices. A `jump across the creek` or a `break of the ice` is an event with price/volume confirmation, not just a line cross.

## 24. In-Gear Relative Strength

Strong candidates often show three things together:

- absolute trend strength
- relative strength versus peer group
- relative strength versus benchmark

Fraser treats this `in gear` condition as a powerful ranking filter.

## 25. Conservative Distribution Counting

One practical Fraser-style PnF heuristic is to undercount distribution rather than overcount it, and in some structures count from `LPSY` back to `ST` instead of all the way to the climax when that produces a more realistic objective.
