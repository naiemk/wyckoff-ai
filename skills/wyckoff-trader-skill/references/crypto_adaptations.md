# Crypto Adaptations

This file captures the parts of the Wyckoff Analytics crypto archive that materially adapt classical Wyckoff logic to digital-asset markets.

## 1. Intermarket Context Is A Hard Gate

Repeated archive theme:

- Bitcoin and major crypto structures often cannot be read in isolation.
- S&P and Nasdaq frequently act as gating variables, especially in risk-on/risk-off transitions.
- During stress, a clean crypto structure can fail simply because stock-index selling reasserts itself.

Practical use:

- A crypto scenario should include the relevant intermarket dependency explicitly.
- If the setup depends on broad risk appetite, say so.
- If macro is mixed, downgrade confidence rather than forcing a high-conviction call.

## 2. Bitcoin Is Both Leader And Funding Source

The archive repeatedly shows two different Bitcoin roles:

- `leader`: strongest and safest crypto expression during early recovery or risk-off repair
- `funding source`: liquid asset sold to raise cash during exogenous shocks or forced liquidation

Implication:

- A violent Bitcoin selloff is not always pure distribution.
- In panic conditions, ask whether Bitcoin is being liquidated for cash rather than structurally rejected.

## 3. Crypto Rotation Is Internal And Layered

The archive treats crypto as a rotational ecosystem, not a monolith.

Important rotational buckets:

- Bitcoin
- large caps
- mid caps
- low caps / micro caps
- exchange tokens
- thematic groups like DeFi
- spread charts such as `ETHBTC`

Recurring heuristics:

- risk-off: money hides in Bitcoin and sometimes Tether
- improving risk appetite: capital rotates from Bitcoin into larger altcoins
- later-stage speculation: capital reaches low caps and thematic groups
- terminal behavior: Bitcoin may surge while altcoins lag, then altcoins catch up during Bitcoin pauses

## 4. Spread Charts Are For Detection, Not Direct Execution

Archive rule:

- Use spread charts like `LINKBTC` or `ETHBTC` to detect comparative strength and early rotation.
- Do not blindly trade the spread chart itself when the base asset environment is poor.
- Once leadership is detected, move back to the tradable USD/perp chart to locate the real point of entry.

This is one of the most useful crypto-specific workflow adaptations in the corpus.

## 5. Comparative Strength Often Beats Absolute Structure

In crypto, the best structural setup is often not the best trade.

The archive favors:

- structurally sound assets that are also outperforming peers
- leadership confirmed through cap-tier indexes or spread charts
- avoiding technically acceptable but lagging names

Use cases:

- pick long candidates from the leading sector or index
- pick shorts from weak laggards if macro weakens

## 6. Low-Liquidity Markets Need More Scenario Tolerance

Crypto often displays:

- deeper shakeouts
- repeated tests
- climactic tails
- local overthrows and underthrows
- sharper intraday reversals

As a result:

- single-signal certainty is dangerous
- alternate scenarios are mandatory
- tests and failed signals matter more than textbook-perfect labels

## 7. Failed Signals Matter More Than Usual

The archive repeatedly elevates the `failed signal` as a decisive clue.

Examples of meaning:

- failed upthrust -> demand is stronger than expected
- failed short trigger -> bearish thesis is likely wrong or early
- failed spring -> bullish read is suspect and may turn into weakness

Rule:

- When a failure occurs at a planned level, update the scenario immediately.
- Do not cling to the original narrative after a meaningful failed signal.

## 8. Action -> Test -> Confirmation Is Preferred

Repeated campaign guidance:

- do not chase the first structural hint
- prefer the full sequence:
  - action
  - test
  - confirmation

Common expressions in the archive:

- spring -> test -> breakout
- MSOS -> backing up -> continuation
- supply event -> retest -> reversal or continuation

This is especially important in crypto because first breaks are frequently noisy.

## 9. Feathers, Hinges, Flat Reactions, And Compression Matter

Archive-specific tactical language adds useful texture:

- `bullish feather`: higher prices or higher highs on decreasing volume into resistance, often preceding acceleration
- `hinge`: tightening structure that can signal an important directional release
- `flat reaction`: limited pullback after strength, often bullish
- `principle in the principle`: a smaller range or reaction nested inside a larger one that refines timing

These patterns are best used as modifiers, not as standalone edge.

## 10. Three Stages Of Uptrend Help In Crypto

The archive applies a practical uptrend sequence:

- value buying / initial momentum
- absorption
- speculation

Why it matters:

- speculation stage often invites late crowd participation
- terminal altcoin rotation frequently appears late in this sequence
- if Bitcoin is only in the absorption stage, a much larger move may still remain

## 11. Thematic Indexes Can Lead Before Single Names

The archive sometimes reads:

- DeFi indexes
- low-cap or mid-cap indexes
- exchange-token group behavior

Benefit:

- group leadership can appear before the best individual chart becomes obvious
- thematic strength can help rank which single assets deserve deeper Wyckoff study

## 12. Halving And Catalysts Are Timing Modifiers, Not Primary Causes

Important nuance from the archive:

- events like the Bitcoin halving do not mechanically cause price appreciation
- instead, they can coincide with or catalyze an already-building accumulation campaign

Use this as a filter:

- catalysts support a structural thesis
- catalysts do not replace structural evidence

## 13. Historical Analogs Are Useful, But Only As Behavioral Templates

The archive uses:

- 1987 analogs
- 1998 bottom analogs
- 2017 China-ban analogs
- prior Ethereum and ChainLink structures

Best use:

- compare sequence and behavior, not exact shape
- analogs are scenario aids, not predictions

## 14. Practical Crypto-Specific Scenario Checklist

Before finalizing any crypto scenario, answer:

1. What is the higher-timeframe cycle and phase?
2. What is S&P/Nasdaq doing, and does it matter here?
3. Is Bitcoin leading, lagging, or being used as a funding source?
4. Where is capital rotating inside crypto?
5. Is this asset relatively strong or weak versus its benchmark?
6. Has the key signal been tested?
7. What failed-signal behavior would immediately change the thesis?
